Mission

For my community

My mission is to build a community-driven platform that helps entrepreneurs achieve economic freedom through their sheer brilliance, collaboration, and hard-work. This platform empowers its community members to produce capital by providing affordable and accessible information technology, financial, recruiting, consulting, product incubation, and business development services. This collective of talented individuals and set of curated services reduces the risk of starting a business by efficiently sharing knowledge, sales, and operational overhead. My goal is to empower working-class entrepreneurs to establish profitable, socially responsible businesses while maintaining significant ownership and creative control.

For my clients

My mission is to make healthcare more affordable and accessible by disrupting the economic systems that fail to serve the general public. I aim to address harmful systemic issues that hinder life science research organizations and healthcare providers from effectively solving pressing human health problems. The consulting services I provide and product startups I support empower neuroscience researchers and mental health clinicians with emerging technology, breakthrough business models, and strategic partnerships. Despite existing broken financial and healthcare systems, my clients and I will work together to improve the quality of life by reducing the prevalence of neurodegenerative diseases and generational trauma.

Vision

For my community

I envision a partnership ecosystem of small, privately-owned, self-funded technology consulting companies, open-source software vendors, and decentralized scientific research organizations that share knowledge, talent, sales, tools, and other resources efficiently. This massive collective of businesses will compete against incumbent large corporations by partnering together to create greater economies of scale than if they were working independently.

These businesses will build talent communities focused on human warmth and emotional connection. Their talent will receive higher pay per hour worked and have better job security than their peers at incumbent competitors. Members of this community will own multiple businesses, generate multiple revenue streams for themselves, and share in the profit of their own enterprise. This partnership ecosystem will be a magnet for top performing talent, providing our customers and clients with high quality products and services, increasing sales and revenue for all businesses in the ecosystem, resulting in greater compensation, benefits, and freedoms that will in turn attract more exceptional talent.

Through emerging technology, cooperative economic principles, and collective bargaining power, my partners and I will disrupt systemic wealth inequities and change the fundamental nature of work.

For my clients

I envision a world without disease where mental health disorders are viewed as forms of diversity instead of as problems to solve. This burgeoning society is fully inclusive of neurodiverse people, with accessible and personalized healthcare and workplaces the norm. We all have everything we need, live as long as we want, remain physically strong forever, and feel emotionally safe everywhere.

Freedoms

The Freedom to Share Business Infrastructure

The most important freedom to our shared success as a community of entrepreneurs and business partners is the freedom to share and collaborate on business infrastructure.

This infrastructure includes ideas, designs, processes, tools, talent, and anything else that can be leveraged to create capital. By following cooperative economic principles, coordinating and focusing our efforts, and efficiently helping to scale each other’s businesses, we will all be more successful.

Expand to see these freedoms
  1. The freedom to use shared infrastructure as you wish, for any purpose, as long as the purpose is aligned to my values and these freedoms. For example, the freedom to incubate your own business in my community and on my platforms without having to share ownership with me.
  1. The freedom to study how our shared infrastructure works, and to change it so it works for you. As examples, I will be transparent in my finances and give you access to the source code of any software tools I write. You can then build modified versions of these financial and software systems that work better for you and others.
  1. The freedom to share this infrastructure with others that hold our shared values and provide these same freedoms. This is the freedom to cooperate.
  1. The freedom to share modified versions of our infrastructure with others. By doing this you can give the whole community a chance to benefit from your changes.

These freedoms will prevent our business from becoming an instrument of unjust power that manipulates and controls other people. Let’s collaborate on, and openly share, business infrastructure so we can achieve economic freedom together.

The Freedom of Consent at Work (WIP)

The most important freedom we have as individuals is the freedom to spend time in ways that give us joy.

When working with me, you are free to use your time to pay attention to what matters to you. A key business outcome of the platforms I am building is to ask for consent in how people work — when, where, on what, and with whom — plus, to some extent, how much they work for. This will help us build warm connections and trusted relationships with each other which will lead to all of us living happy, fulfilled lives.

We also need recognize that if we set too many ultimatums or act too self-righteous, it simply makes it hard to work together. We still have lots of work to do to build a platform to fully realize these freedoms (aka “desire is not a strategy”). To make it easy to work together, balancing mutual consent with a well-executed, community-wide strategy is necessary.

I ask for consent in:
  1. How one works. This involves respecting each other’s boundaries and actively weaving our principles and processes together. I respect the way other people work through an adaptive leadership style while encouraging the use of standard project management methodologies (like software development lifecycles) and operating procedures (like sales outreach processes). This combination of leadership approaches allows us to balance autonomy with predictable routines and creativity with operational efficiency.
  1. What you work on. A person should never be forced to work on a project that doesn’t align to their values. At the same time, we need to demonstrate awareness on how choosing to do different things divides us and makes it harder to achieve shared goals. I will practice active listening during moments when our work is misaligned and use the feedback to create a better strategy.
  1. Who you work with. We each work best with different people. We want team compositions to consider personality differences so each team can work most effectively together. If an individual is not a good fit for my team, I will actively work to help them find the team that is best for them.
  1. When you work. Our individual lives are full of all kinds of commitments and desires that exist outside of the scope of work at one organization. These can range from family duties, like picking kids up from daycare or a mother needing time to breastfeed, or a person following their dreams to be a professional musician or to start their own business. I support individuals living the life they want to live with the assumption that they will help coordinate and focus our collective efforts. For us to be successful as a team, we need to show discipline (aka you gotta show up), commitment (aka you gotta keep your word), and persistence (aka you gotta never give up) when working together.
  1. Where you work. Because most of my collaborators are building digital worlds, they are not locked down to physical spaces. I see the benefits of working together in a shared physical space, like how it improves empathy and alignment, but will never require someone to work where I work. This allows those around me to be efficient with their time (like by not commuting long distances), to live their values (like by not driving and contributing to emissions), to have a unique lifestyle (like by wanting to work from anywhere around the world), and to go on adventures (like to go on tour as a musician).
  1. How much you get paid. I always ask how much someone wants to get paid and will accommodate that rate if it is affordable and reasonable. If I can’t afford the rate, I am honest and transparent about my finances and budgetary constraints.
  1. Why you work. I won’t judge folks for why they are working on projects with me. If we’re working well together and meeting shared business objectives, the reasons don’t matter. If our purpose aligns, great, but if they simply want to make money, have fun, and learn, that’s cool too.

Talking about consent is an important part of any healthy relationship. It’s an easy way to make sure nobody is being pressured into something they don’t want to do. This not only makes life and work more fun for individuals, but also benefits our businesses by encouraging engagement, creativity, communication, collaboration, and productivity. We are optimizing for the whole of the collective while respecting each individual’s needs.

I am blocked by slow decision making models. In particular, regarding how money is invested and how we align the efforts of groups larger than two or three people. We are starting to have solutions defined to our various cold start problems and now are struggling to scale.

More independence, free play, and responsibility in the real world

Many parents are afraid to give their children the level of independence and responsibility they themselves enjoyed when they were young, even though rates of homicide, drunk driving, and other physical threats to children are way down in recent decades. Part of the fear comes from the fact that parents look at each other to determine what is normal and therefore safe, and they see few examples of families acting as if a 9-year-old can be trusted to walk to a store without a chaperone. But if many parents started sending their children out to play or run errands, then the norms of what is safe and accepted would change quickly. So would ideas about what constitutes “good parenting.” And if more parents trusted their children with more responsibility––for example, by asking their kids to do more to help out, or to care for others––then the pervasive sense of uselessness now found in surveys of high-school students might begin to dissipate.

If parents don’t replace screen time with real-world experiences involving friends and independent activity, then banning devices will feel like deprivation, not the opening up of a world of opportunities.

The main reason why the phone-based childhood is so harmful is because it pushes aside everything else. Smartphones are experience blockers. Our ultimate goal should not be to remove screens entirely, nor should it be to return childhood to exactly the way it was in 1960. Rather, it should be to create a version of childhood and adolescence that keeps young people anchored in the real world while flourishing in the digital age.
